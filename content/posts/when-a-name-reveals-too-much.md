A court case about a patient's roommate ended up teaching me more about privacy than I expected.

The case was *Rogers v. NYU Hospitals Center*. A hospital released the name of a patient's roommate and someone questioned if this violated HIPAA. The name came up during a legal proceeding and the patient argued it should have been protected under HIPAA. The court disagreed. The name alone didn't reveal any medical diagnosis or treatment. The Rusk Institute offered a wide range of rehabilitative services, so knowing someone was there didn't tell you anything about their condition. A name by itself wasn't protected health information in that context.

But here's the part that got me thinking. It all comes down to context.

If I were a privacy officer, the places I'd worry about most are the ones where just knowing a patient's name would reveal senstitive information. Specialized facilities like oncology units, HIV treatment centers, dialysis clinics, psychiatric hospitals, substance use programs. In these places, someone's presence alone tells you their diagnosis. That's when identity basically becomes health data.

It gets trickier in rural communities. Smaller populations make it easier to figure out who's receiving what kind of treatment, even in larger hospitals. In those situations, a name has to be treated as protected information.

Large general hospitals, emergency departments, multidisciplinary rehab centers? These are usually less of a concern. They serve so many different kinds of patients that knowing someone was there doesn't really tell you much about their diagnosis or treatment.

Context is everything in privacy work. The same data point creates different risks in different places. Missing this means your privacy controls end up either too loose or pointlessly restrictive.
