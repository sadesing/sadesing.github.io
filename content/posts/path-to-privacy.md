My interest in privacy did not start with some big moment of realization. It built slowly over time, which is usually how the most meaningful shifts happen.

For the past few years I have been working as a frontend engineer focused on accessibility compliance. My background in systems engineering and human factors shaped how I think about technology. I tend to look at systems through the lens of how people actually experience them. Do they support people, or do they quietly make things harder?

Working in accessibility deepened this perspective even more. As I focused on building inclusive experiences, I realized that consent, transparency, and control were just as important as usability. This highlighted how much accessibility and privacy overlap.

Eventually I started noticing privacy issues everywhere. Not in an abstract way, but in everyday situations. Data breaches stopped feeling like distant headlines and started affecting people around me, sometimes even me directly. I began paying closer attention to how companies collect and handle data, how little protection we actually have in the United States compared to places like Europe, and how often personal information gets mishandled or exposed.

Privacy started to matter to me in a way it had not before.

Around the same time, the tech industry was shifting. Layoffs were happening across the industry. AI was starting to change how development work looked. On top of that, the rollback of DEI initiatives was cutting deeply into accessibility roles, which was the area I had been working in.

It became pretty clear the market was narrowing and I needed to start thinking about what came next.

So I took a step back and looked at the skills I had built over the years. My human factors background helped me understand how people interact with technology. Accessibility compliance meant working with regulations, audits, and policy frameworks. Frontend development gave me a practical understanding of how systems are actually built.

When I looked at it that way, moving toward privacy work started to make a lot of sense.

This past summer I began researching programs and eventually came across [Albany Law School's MS in Cybersecurity and Data Privacy](https://www.albanylaw.edu/graduate/online-ms-cybersecurity-and-data-privacy). What stood out to me was the mix of legal foundations and technical coursework. It is also fully online, which makes it possible for me to keep working while studying.

The program felt like the right fit, so I applied, was accepted, and now I am a few weeks into classes.

![Albany Law School acceptance letter dated July 30, 2025](/images/albany-acceptance.png)

I already have one master’s degree, so going back to school was not exactly part of my long term plan. But the more I learn, the more it feels like the right decision.

What draws me to privacy is that it sits at the intersection of technology, law, and ethics. I want to understand how AI is shaping the way data is used, how different countries approach privacy regulation, and what it actually takes to design systems that protect people’s information instead of simply collecting as much as possible.

I do not know exactly where this path leads yet, but I am excited to be on it.

Looking back, the twists and turns in my career have brought me to a place that feels surprisingly aligned. Sometimes the path forward is not the one you originally planned. It only starts to make sense once you look back and see how everything connects.
